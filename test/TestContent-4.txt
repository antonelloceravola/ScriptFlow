
*Title: Emotional Ignorance

:Why We Feel Everything and Understand Almost Nothing

From the moment we wake up, go to work, buy coffee, or meet friends, we experience countless emotions till we go to sleep, but rarely truly invest the time to understand them or use them to improve our life.

:First Edition


*Preface

-Antonello

As a child, I was always curious about people and their behavior. Yet, for reasons I could not fully understand at that time, their behavior often felt difficult for me to interpret. I was one of those children who could easily spend hours absorbed in playing, building, or exploring an idea, and regularly paying too little attention to the subtle signals others were exchanging around me. That distance created a barrier.

At some point during my teenage years, I came across a book that left a deep impression on me: “Your Erroneous Zones” by Wayne Dyer. This book deeply touched me. Transformed me. Among the many ideas it contained, one observation struck me in a particular way. It suggested that emotions often shape our reactions more than we realize. By reflecting on this, I began noticing how frequently my own behavior changed when certain emotions appeared.

In many ways, that question became one of the seeds of this book.

-Jean

I grew up at the crossroads of philosophy and the sciences that explore mind and spirit. My father — chief physician in both hospital and community care, a frontline man — was a professor of psychiatry, and as a child I often found myself close to worlds that “respectable” society prefers to keep at arm’s length: clinics, case files, the fragile architectures of the mind, and the social stigma borne by that profoundly civilized “people of the mad”. 

I was a boy and, like many boys, I was seized by an impossible desire. I told an old Buryat sage — someone my father respected deeply — that I wanted to become a migratory bird. I did not say it as a metaphor. I said it with the seriousness children reserve for their most sincere truths.


*Introduction: Breaking the Mask

We have all heard the idea that everyone wears a mask in public. A familiar story, repeated many times. The idea that we present a public version of ourselves to the world — more controlled, more acceptable, more protected — while keeping our fragile, uncertain, or vulnerable parts hidden inside.

This book looks at a different kind of mask. A mask can not be taken off.
Not one we put on in front of others, but one we slowly grow over time. A mask made of habits, expectations, and learned reactions. Layer after layer, it forms in response to family, school, work, and society — not to deceive, but to function. And forgetting it is the beginning of our emotional ignorance.

And that is the problem.

-The Mask We Grow

When we are born, we are direct. Not naïve. Not empty. Just direct. We cry when something hurts. We smile when something feels good. We reach out when we want closeness, and we pull away when we don’t. There is no strategy in it. No performance. No interpretation. What happens inside us appears immediately on the outside.
The mask grows thicker.

Not because we want to deceive others. But because we stop looking inside. At some point, we are no longer sure where the mask ends and where we begin. This book starts from there. Not from the idea that we need to remove the mask completely — that would be neither possible nor desirable at this stage — but from the realization that we have grown one, and that it may be preventing us from understanding ourselves. Because without access to what happens inside us, we have no real way to explain what we do. And without understanding what we do, change remains accidental.

-What the Mask Hides (and How It Traps Us)

The mask does not simply hide something. It changes the way we operate. Once the mask has grown enough, we start living in a subtle loop that is hard to notice while we are inside it. The more we rely on the mask to navigate the world, the less attention we give to what happens beneath it. And the less we look inside, the less familiar our own inner landscape becomes. 
Over time, this creates a loop.

The more we act through the mask, the less we understand what we feel.
 The less we understand what we feel, the more our behavior becomes automatic.
 The more automatic our behavior becomes, the more we rely on the mask to function.

And so the distance grows. Another effect slowly emerges. We don’t just show others something that is not fully us. We start responding to how we think others see us. We anticipate reactions. We adjust in advance. We perform versions of ourselves that feel appropriate, safe, or expected. Then we react to the feedback we receive — feedback that is already filtered through the mask.

Not as a dramatic loss of control, but as a gradual dimming of awareness. We function. We perform. We respond. But presence fades. Attention narrows. Conscious choice becomes rare. We are awake, but not fully aware. The mask doesn’t just protect us from the world. It protects us from ourselves. And without access to what happens inside us, even our own behavior starts to feel confusing. This is not a personal failure. It is the natural outcome of growing up without ever being taught how to observe our inner world.

-Emotional Ignorance

At this point, it may be tempting to talk about emotional intelligence. About skills. Techniques. Regulation. Control. But that would be skipping a step. Because before intelligence, there is something else. Something more basic. More uncomfortable. More honest.
There is ignorance.
Emotional ignorance does not mean a lack of emotions. It does not mean insensitivity, coldness, or weakness. On the contrary. Most people feel deeply. Intensely. Constantly. Emotional ignorance means something simpler and more subtle: we experience emotions every day, but we rarely recognize them as such. We live with them, react to them, are driven by them — without naming them, without understanding their role, without noticing their influence. They shape our decisions, our relationships, our thoughts, often without ever entering our awareness. Not because we are incapable. But because no one ever taught us how to look.

We learn how to behave.
 We learn how to perform.
 We learn how to succeed, how to adapt, how to meet expectations.
